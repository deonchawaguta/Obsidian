## Global Privacy Laws 
- Almost every country in the world has enact some form of data privacy law with more recent laws focusing on the application of new technologies as it relates to protecting personal data
- As a rule of thumb, anyone trying to solicit business in a country is subject to its privacy laws 
- Two main privacy frameworks influenced many of the world's data protection laws i.e., [[OECD Guidelines on the Protection of Privacy and Transborder Flows of Personal Data]] and the Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation Privacy Framework 
- These principles include collection limitation, data quality, purpose specification, use limitation, security safeguards, openness, individual participation and accountability

### EU General Data Protection Regulation

- Offers a framework for data protection with increased accountability for organisations

#### Article 1: Subject Matter Objectives 
1. The protection of natural persons and their personal data
2. The protection of persons' rights and freedoms 

#### Article 2: Material Scope
1. Territorial Scope - Applies to the processing of personal data by automated means and as part of a filing system
2. Excludes activity that falls outside EU law and performed as part of personal or household activity
3. Excludes law enforcement

#### Article 3: Territorial Scope
The GDPR applies in these three solutions giving it "global scope"
1. **EU Establishment** - If a company has a physical office or "establishment" in the EU, the GDPR applies to their data activities, even if the actual processing happens elsewhere
2. **Targeting EU Residents** - If a company is outside the EU  but **offers goods/services** (even free ones) to people in the EU
3. **Monitoring Behaviour** - If a non-EU company **tracks the behaviour** of people in the EU

### China's Personal Information Protection Law
- An omnibus law that resembles the EU GDPR but does not restrict the PRC's central government from accessing data. 
- Fines for violation can be up to maximum $7.7 million or 5% previous year revenue

## Cross-Border Data Transfers 
- As a general rule, transferring personal data to countries outside the EEA may take place if these countries are deemed to provide an adequate level of data protection 
>[!Tip] Article 45 of the EU GDPR provides the third countries' level of personal data protection as assessed by the European Commission
- For countries not deemed adequate by the EC, standard contractual clauses or SCCs, are a valid method of data transfer.
- Organisations using SCCs should perform **Data Transfer Impact Assessments** on a case-by-case basis. 

>[!Note] To properly perform a DTIA, several steps should be taken:
> - Map where your data is and where it is transferred to
> - Identify the mechanism used for the transfer
> - Assess effectiveness of transfer mechanism 
> - Adopt additional safeguards as needed
> - Ensure additional measures align with business requirements
> - Monitor for ongoing compliance 

- Regulators also expect TIAs to include the following questions:
	- What is the likelihood of government access to the data?
	- Is in the data within the scope of intelligence and law enforcement activities?
	- What are the applicable privacy and security standards of the receiving country?
	- What the general human rights ratings of the receiving country?

### Adequacy Decision
- Adequacy is based on equivalence, there must be an adequate level of protection of personal data equivalent to that provided in the EU
- Where an adequacy decision can't be reached, appropriate safeguards can be adopted to protect personal data and facilitate transfers. 
	
- Examples of these safeguards include:

#### Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs)
- Allow large multinationals to adopt a policy suite with binding rules for handling personal data
#### Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)
- SCCs in Art. 46(c)
- Model contracts in Recital 109
- Codes of conduct or certification mechanisms
- Approved codes of conduct

>[!Important] As a general rule, adjust the the privacy program to the most stringent legal requirements to which the data processing is subject 

## Organisational Balance and Support 
- Compliance should be the baseline 
- PbD will further the organisation's goals and help strike a balance 
- Compliance creates an opportunity to simultaneously reevaluate and improve data management practices 
- Compliance should be achieved with the least amount of business disruption
