## Data Subject Rights 
- In general, legal compliance issues impose 5 categories of requirements on organizations
	- Prevent harm to data subjects
	- Limiting the collection of personal information
	- Providing accountability for data controller and processors
	- Monitoring and enforcing compliance with requirements
	- Requiring reporting of privacy incidents 

### Access 
- This right requires that individuals must be provided with access to and copies of any information the organization maintains about them

### Managing Data Integrity 
- Data subjects must have the ability to redress inaccuracies in their data by requesting that records be corrected 

### Erasure 
- Commonly known as the *right to be forgotten*, this allows individuals to request PII maintained about them to be erased
- The GDPR also requires the controller to pass along the request for erasure 
##### Exceptions to the Right to Erasure
- Exercising the right of freedom of expression and information
- For the performance of a task in the public interest or in the exercise of official authority 
- For reason of public interest in the area of public health
- For archiving purposes in the public interest, scientific, historical or statistical research purposes 
- for the establishment, exercise or defence of legal claims 

### Right to be Informed
- Data subjects have a right to be informed about circumstances where they request erasure, correction or restriction on processing

### Control over Use
- Data subjects have the right to request that controllers stop or restrict processing of their information under certain circumstances 

### Complaints 
- Data subjects have the right to file complaints with government agencies when they believe their privacy rights have been violated

### Handling Information Requests 
- A *personal information inventory* is a good tool to make responding to requests more efficient 

## Incident Response Planning
- The organization must be prepared to conduct a coordinated response effort 

### Stakeholder Identification
- Some key business stakeholders include:
	 - Privacy professionals 
	 - Information security professionals
	 - Legal teams 
	 - Audit teams 
	 - HR 
	 - Marketing and business development 
	 - Communications and PR 

### Building an Incident Oversight Team 
- Organizations should establish a *privacy incident oversight team* responsible for managing the response to privacy incidents 
- Management should be actively involved in the team to provide authority, resources and time

### Building the Incident Response Plan 

#### Policy 
- Should be written at a high level to guide efforts and provide authority for incident response
- The policy is not the place to describe specific technologies, tools and procedures 

- NIST recommends that incident response plans contain the following at minimum
	- Statement of management commitment 
	- Purpose and objectives of the policy 
	- Scope of the policy 
	- Definition of Incidents and related terms
	- Organizations structure and definition of roles 
	- Prioritization or severity rating for incidents 
	- Performance measures
	- Reporting and contact forms 

#### Procedures and Playbooks 
- *Procedures* provide detailed tactical information that team members need 
- *Playbooks* define specific procedures that are taken in the event of a certain type of incident 

### Integrating the Plan with other Functions 
- The plan should be integrate with other business functions, typically because privacy incidents are usually cybersecurity incidents 

## Incident Detection

### Security and Privacy Incidents 
- Cybersecurity incidents involve the compromise of a technology system while privacy incidents involve the unauthorized access of PII

### Security Events and Incidents 
- An *event* is any observable occurrence in a system or network 
- An *adverse event* is any event that has negative consequences 
- A *security incident* is a violation or threat of violation of computer security policies  

### Privacy Incidents 
- *Privacy incidents* occur when an organization fails to meet its privacy obligations under legal or regulatory requirements 

### Reporting Privacy Incidents 
- Organizations may have an obligations to report privacy incidents to data subjects and regulators 
- GDPR Article 33 requires reporting to authorities 
- GDPR Article 34 requires reporting to data subjects 

### Coordination and Information Sharing

#### Internal Communications 
- Should take place over secure channels that are tested for security
#### External Communications
- Types of external communication include:
	- Law enforcement may be involved if the incident is criminal in nature 
	- Information sharing partners provide community based warnings of cybersecurity risk 
	- Vendors may provide information crucial to the response 
	- Other organizations may be victims of the same attack and the organizations may desire to coordinate on the response with them 
	- Communication with the media and public may be mandatory under regulatory requirements
### Breach Notification
-  Many laws and regulations mandate that organizations make public notifications and disclosures in the even of a data breach involving PII 
- Every US state has a data breach notification law but there is no federal law for the same 

## Incident Handling 

### Risk Assessment 
- Firsts step is to identify the potential harm caused to the organization and data subjects 
- The risk assessment should examine:
	- The nature of the PII involved
	- Risk of harm to data subjects and the organization
	- Likelihood of unauthorized access to PII
	- Number of individuals affected 

### Containment Activities
- Containment should begin as quickly as possible, it is a critical priority 
- These activities should isolate the incident and prevent it from spreading 

### Remediation Measures
- This phase focuses on restoring normal capabilities and services  
- During remediation, the incident's *root cause* should be determined
