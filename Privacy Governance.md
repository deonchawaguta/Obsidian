- Building a privacy program requires the establishments of appropriate governance. 
- *Privacy Governance* generally refers to components that guide a privacy functions toward compliance with privacy laws and regulations

>[!Important] The components of Privacy Governance are:
> - Privacy Vision and Mission Statement
> - Privacy program scope 
> - Privacy framework 
> - Organisational privacy strategy 
> - Privacy team structure

### Privacy Vision and Mission Statement
- A 'Privacy Vision Statement' should align with the organisation's broader purpose and business objectives 
- A 'Privacy Mission Statement' describes the purpose and ideas in just a few sentences. 


### Privacy Program Scope
- The scope of a privacy program can be established using one of two approaches:
	1. Identifying the personal information collected and processed
	2. Identifying applicable privacy and data protection laws and regulations 

#### Identify the Personal Information Collected and Processed 
- A first step can be holding information gathering interviews with the functions that typically store and process personal information-HR, marketing, finance, etc. 
- Engaging internal audit, data governance or information security may also assist with data discovery 
- A more automated approach involves engaging third parties such as consultancies or data discovery tools, such engagements are usually more robust and needed for complex data environments 

>[!Note] Key questions to ask when defining scope include:
> - Who collects, uses and maintains personal information?
> - What types of personal information are collected and for what purpose?
> - Where is the data stored? 
> - To whom is the data transferred?
> - Who has access to the data? Internally and externally 
> - When is the data collected?
> - How long is the data retained? How is it disposed?
> - What security controls are in place to protect the data? 

#### In-Scope Privacy and Data Protection Laws and Regulations
- After identifying metadata about the collected personal information, the next step is to identify the obligations related to that data. 

>[!Note] If there are inadequate or no data protection regulations, institute requirements, policies and procedures to the highest level achievable. Choose the *most* not the least restrictive practices
>

### Challenges
- Domestic privacy programs may only need to monitor state or regional laws as well as industry specific regulations 
- Global programs may have to take cultural norms into account as well 
- Determining the applicability of international data protection laws is the responsibility of the privacy professional 

#### Approaches to Privacy and Data Protection Around the Globe 
- United States 
	- *Sectoral Laws* - Laws to specifically address a particular industry sector
		- Financial Transactions
		- Medical Records 
	- *State-Specific Laws* - Laws that apply to the residents of a specific state
		- California CCPA 
		- Virginia CDPA 
- EU Member States, Canada, United Kingdom
	- *Comprehensive Laws* - Govern the use of personal information in public and private sectors with an official oversight body
		- EU GDPR
- Australia 
	- *Co-Regulatory Model* - Variant of the comprehensive model where industry develops enforcement standards that are overseen by a privacy agency 
- United States, Singapore
	- *Self-Regulated Model* - Companies use a code of practice by a group of companies known as industry bodies 

>[!Note] A successful scoping approach involves:
> - Understanding of personal data life cycle 
> - Consideration of the global perspective to meet legal, cultural and personal expectations 
> - Customising privacy approached for local and global perspectives 
> - Monitoring of legal compliance factors for both global and local markets 

### Developing a Privacy Strategy
- **A *Privacy Strategy* is an organisation's approach to communication and supporting the privacy program and its vision
- There is no one size fits all solution for a privacy strategy 
- Developing a strategy requires all staff to understand and employ practices for protecting personal data. 

#### Identifying Stakeholders and Internal Partnerships 
- Building support requires visibility of the privacy initiative, this can be achieved first by holding informal conversations with members of organisations management 
- It is also important to select a privacy champion at the executive level who will act as an advocate and a sponsor 

>[!Important] Best practices when building internal partnerships include:
> - Become aware of how personal information is viewed and treated by others
> - Understand their use of data in a business context
> - Assist with building privacy requirements in ongoing projects 
> - Offer to help staff meet their objectives while offering solutions to reduce risk of personal information exposure
> - Invite staff to be part of a privacy advocacy group 


#### Conduct a Privacy Workshop for Stakeholders
- With the support of the program sponsor, conduct workshops for the stakeholders who will support efforts to develop and launch a privacy program 
- Explain market expectations, answer questions and reduce confusion 

#### Keep a Record of Ownership 
- Form a steering committee to ensure clear ownership of assets and responsibilities and document it
- Such documentation can support accountability requirements

### Develop and Implement a Framework 
- Develop an approach to operationalising the controls needed to protect personal information
- An appropriate privacy framework can:
	- Help achieve material compliance with in-scope laws and regulations
	- Serve as a competitive advantage by reflecting the value of personal information
	- Support business commitment and objectives 

### Frameworks 
- The term refers broadly to the various processes, templates, tools, laws and standards that may guide the privacy program's management 
- Frameworks primarily answer the following questions:
	- Are risks identified and defined?
	- Has the program been implemented in work-streams?
	- Are gaps understood?
	- Is privacy management monitored?
	- Are employees trained?
	- Are industry best practices for data inventories and assessments followed?
	- Does the organisation have an incident response plan?
	- Does the organisation communicate privacy matter and update material as needed?
	- Does the organisation use a common language to address and manage cybersecurity risk based on business and organisation needs?

	#### Principles and Standards
- **[[Fair Information Practice Principles]]** 
	- Provide basic privacy principles central to several modern frameworks
- **OECD Guidelines on the Protection of Privacy and Transborder Flows of Personal Data
	- The most widely accepted set of privacy principles 
- **[[Generally Accepted Privacy Principles]]** 
	- Developed to guide organisations in developing, implementing and managing privacy program in line with laws and best practices
- **Canadian Standards Association Privacy Code**
	- A national standard that later formed the basis for PIPEDA
- **[[NIST Privacy Framework]]**
	- A framework adaptable to any organisation's role in the data protection ecosystem
	- A risk-based, customisable framework that splits its components into three parts 
		- A "core" set of activities 
		- "Profiles", sets of core activities that organisations are expected to customise based on various factors 
		- "Tiers" that consider the level of operations maturity achievable for a given profile, based on key criteria
- **[[Privacy by Design]]** 
	- An approach to privacy program development based on a set of *seven foundational principles*
	- The intent is to ensure every stage of the development life cycle takes into account privacy requirements
	
>[!Note] PbD Principles
> - Proactive not reactive 
> - Privacy as the default setting
> - Full functionality 
> - End-to-end security 
> - Visibility and Transparency
> - Respect for user privacy 

#### Laws, Regulations and Programs
- **Canada's Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act** provides examples of generic privacy principles through national laws 
- **EU GDPR** offers a framework for data protection with increased obligations for organisations and far-reaching effects 
- **EU-US Privacy Shield** establishes a cross-border data transfer mechanism between the two regions, replacing the previous Safe Harbour Framework
- **Binding Corporate Rules** are legally binding internal corporate privacy rules for the transferring personal information with a corporate group, *EU GDPR* Article 47 lists the requirement of BCRs
- **HIPAA** is a US law passed to create national standards for electronic health care transactions, it required the US Department of Health to establish regulations to protect the privacy and security of personal health information

#### Rationalising Requirements 
- This phrase means implementing a solution that materially addresses privacy requirements 
- At a high level, most data privacy legislation imposes the same obligations on regulated entities 
- A rationalised approach also requires addressing requirements outside the common obligations on a case-by-case basis

#### Governance Models 
- Positioning of the privacy team within the organisation should rely on the authority it will receive under the governance model being followed 
- Privacy governance models include centralised, local and hybrid versions but are not limited only to these options. 

##### Centralised 
- Fits well in organisations used to utilising single-channel functions 
- Planning and decision-making are completed by one group
- One team is responsible for privacy-related affairs

##### Local or Decentralised 
- Delegating decision-making authority to the lower levels in an organisation
- Likely to run on less rigid policies and wider spans of control among each officer of the organisation
- One advantage is bottom-up flow of information, decisions about lower level operations are well informed

##### Hybrid
- A combination of centralised and local governance 
- A main individual or departments is assigned responsibility for privacy-related affairs and issuing directives 
- Local entities fulfil and support the policies and directives from the governing body
- The model will dictate core values and leave the details of implementation to individual employees

#### Advantages and Disadvantages 
- Centralised management offers streamlined processes and procedures while requiring that individual employees seek approval from higher levels for actions related to privacy matter 
- Decentralised management may be inefficient as processes may need to be reproduced many times instead of once 
- The hybrid model encourages a sense of ownership in employees
